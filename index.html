Make A Portrait of Childhood Self, 2024
Help people embrace their vulnerability through self-portrait making

This design is an interactive installation aimed at enabling participants to create self-portraits guided by their childhood memories. This project draws inspiration from the modern artwork that explores self-image and the therapeutic effect of art therapy, aiming to help people accept their vulnerable feelings through introspection and creative activity. To achieve this, expert interviews with professionals and exploration with generative models were conducted to gather insights and inform the design process. The installation incorporates prompting guidance that evoke personal childhood memories, fostering an emotional connection and self-reflection. The findings indicates that the installation succeeded in providing playable experiences and eliciting positive emotions for people, which is very beneficial in cultivating self-acceptance and helping people embrace their vulnerability.

Fascinating art form     

Painting, with its extensive history, stands out as a
particularly popular medium for self-portraits. "Man in
a Red Turban" by Jan van Eyck (1433) (Fig. 1) is often
considered one of the earliest examples of a panel
self-portrait.[9] The portrait is notable for its
remarkable attention to detail, capturing realistic
textures like the turban, and the meticulous portrayal
of the man's facial features[15].

Frida's “self-portrait with cropped hair” (1940).(Fig. 2)
Self- portraits can be very emotionally expressive,
serving as a format for artists to convey their
identities, and momentary emotions, or engage in social
commentary. In this particular self-portrait, Kahlo
diverges from her typical depictions, shedding the
feminine attributes often present in her work, such
as traditional embroidered Tehuana dresses or flowers
in her hair. Instead, she boldly presents herself in a
loose-fitting man's suit with a short-cropped haircut.
The floor is strewn with locks of her hair, and she
holds a pair of shears across her lap. This androgynous
portrayal is thought to reflect Kahlo's bisexuality and
her response to a divorce from her husband [23]

Various media

Self-portraits extend beyond paintings
into various media, and the emergence of
photographs lowers the thresholds of
creating self-portraits. photograph became
one of the most popular media of self-
portrait. Cindy Sherman "Untitled
#153" (1985). (Fig. 3) This photograph is
seen as an exploration of pop culture
clichés related to women, delving into the
historical and contemporary roles of women
in society. It serves as a reflection of
Sherman's strong and deliberate identity
as a woman [13].

As a similar medium to photograph, self-
portraits are also created in videos. An
inspiring example is the video “Selfportrait”
with cartoon technique by Maria Lassnig.(Fig. 4)
The drawing is the author's face in a comic-
like style, which explore themes of identity
and self-perception with a cartoonish aesthetic.

Facial features

Artists sometimes employ distorted human faces in self-
portraits, such as those by Pablo Picasso(Fig. 5) or
Francis Bacon.(Fig. 6) In photography, Cindy Sherman's
"Untitled #414" (Fig. 7) features a self-portrait with
exaggerated costume and makeup, diminishing her physical
likeness.

These distorted face images are conveying even stronger
expression of the artist's identity and emotions.

Identity explora

Text-based portraits, for instance,
completely give up images, leaving ample
room for readers to imagine and construct
images of the authors through their
interpretation. Self-portraits in
performance art share similarities with
photography by abandoning the static and
eternal for the dynamic and temporary.
Anderson Butler's performance pieces,
described as self-portraits using new
media, exemplify this approach. Here,
the audience's perception of the artist's
self-image is derived not from their face
but from their performance.

Soundscape or voice recording self-
portraits also exist. Laurie Anderson is
a particularly innovative artist in this
medium. In her 2004 performance piece
"The End of the Moon," Anderson narrates
personal stories with violin music,
creating a self-portrait through her
narratives and music, providing insights
into her life, beliefs, and experiences.

Self-portraits, even without the artist's
facial image, are considered self-
portraits due to their strong expression
of the artist's identity. This demonstrates
that depicting physical characteristics is
not essential for an artwork to be
identified as a self-portrait. Creating
these self-portraits in various media
involves an intense process of self-
reflection on the artist's self-image.

Therapeutic Function of Self-Portrait Making -- click to expand

▼

The therapeutic function of self-portraits is obvious, establishing a close connection between self-portraiture and therapy.
- Self-portrait as a kind of art is often produced by the raw material “emotions”. As the subjects of their own artistic creations, we become unavoidably vulnerable, people are forced to think about how they want to be presented in the portrait, and that elicits self-awareness and a process of self-reflection.
- The inner dialogue that takes place in front of the camera or the canvas or the recorder etc. mirrors the internal processes encountered in therapy — self-perception, self-questioning, judgment, thoughtful contemplation, and acceptance.
- Visual, audio, or text-based self-portraits (as opposed to a self-image existing solely in the mind) are indispensable. They serve as tangible reflections on various aspects of one's being. Creating a self-portrait may unveil a hidden self and having a self-portrait that people can reflect on later might provide a new perspective on viewing themselves so that they can discover different facets of themselves.
- It's also obvious that every self-portrait is always a form of performance. Individuals engage in a deliberate "performance" to present a desired image, which resembles role-playing in theatrical dramas. The unique combination of self-subjectivity and dramatic features in self-portraiture holds a particular promise in addressing issues related to self-image and feelings of vulnerability.

The Workflow

Interactive Installation That Enables Self-Portrait Making

The final design is an interactive installation featuring a central screen, a webcam above it, two printers on either side, and two main control panels below. The lower control panel includes a eyboard and a purple button-board, while the other is a yellow panel equipped with two rotary switches and three buttons.

There are 10 pairs of quesions in total, they are randomized and one of the pairs is presented in th einitial page at a time. In a question pair, one of the questions is about childhood, and the other is about nowhood:
1. What were you like when you were a child?,
Give 3 adjectives to describe yourself here and now.
2. What makes you happy when you were a child?,
What was something small that made you smile today?
3. Is there a childhood story or movie that still resonates with you today?,
Have you read or watched anything interesting lately that stuck with you?
...
The yellow panel's buttons and rotary switches let users edit the portrait. The 'regenerate' button prompts GPT to create a new text portrait. The 'photograph' button freezes the webcam after a 5-second countdown; pressing it again unfreezes the frame. The 'back to beginning' button resets the webpage.

The yellow panel

The writing style rotary switch offers three options—'short and strong,' 'poetic', and 'story-like'— that modify the prompt sent to GPT, resulting in different text portraits.
